Free Willy and Flipper by the Numbers

By Amber Thomas and Russell Goldenberg

The past, present, and future of captive whales and dolphins according to data and pop culture.

Free Willy is peak ‘90s nostalgia. Who watched this movie and didn’t fall in love with the black and white behemoth? Fans were so inspired, they helped raise the millions of dollars necessary to try to release the orca/actor back into the wild (with questionable success). Unfortunately, the reality and history of captivity is more complex than the movie suggested, and the complexity has only increased in the years since.

But Free Willy wasn’t the first (or the last) movie to surface this topic to mainstream conversation. To clear up some of these complexities, let’s take a brief stroll through the history of dolphin and whale (cetacean) captivity. We will explore the past, present, and future of cetacean captivity in the US, and how it is depicted by data and pop culture.

In order to tell a story about these animals, we first need to understand how they came to be in aquariums in the first place. That story begins back in the late 1930s when cetaceans were captured from the ocean and displayed in first US marine park.

For the next 20 years or so, a handful of animals were captured annually and kept in the US.

But in 1963 "Flipper" hit big screens nationwide and suddenly everyone wanted to see or own a dolphin. More animals were captured so that they could be displayed in aquariums, roadside attractions, restaurants, and even swimming pools.

And at the time, this wasn't shocking. We knew almost nothing about these animals, and often they were considered a nuisance. If you're finding this hard to believe, check out this scene from the 1963 version of Flipper.

At this point, capturing cetaceans was unregulated. The Marine Mammal Protection Act, a law which regulates where, when, and why animals were captured went into effect in December 1972. In the months leading up to the increased regulation, the number of annual captures more than tripled.

Around this same time, researchers were learning more about dolphin intelligence, communication, social structures, and reproduction.

This newfound respect for cetacean intelligence showed up repeatedly in pop culture at the time, but one of the more extreme examples comes from Douglas Adams' "Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy" books. In the stories, dolphins were referred to as the only animals on the planet who knew of our world's coming destruction (and humans were too stupid to take their hints).

By the early 90's, when Free Willy and the Flipper re-make were released, Americans (as a whole) had a different view towards these animals. They weren't nuisance creatures anymore, but intelligent, social animals that shouldn't be captured. And so, without any law restricting it, aquariums in the US stopped capturing wild cetaceans completely.

Once wild-captures had ended, captive breeding became the primary method of gaining new animals in US facilities.

The remaining new admissions were all rescued animals, like Winter, the fluke-less dolphin featured in the 2011 film "Dolphin Tale".

These rescued animals were unable to be released back into the ocean and wouldn't have survived if marine mammal facilities didn't exist.

Where are they now?

Only about 20% of the cetaceans that have ever been in captivity in the US are still alive today. Keep in mind, though, we’re talking about 80 years of cetaceans in human care, and these animals just don’t live that long.

How long do they live? The short answer is that, on average, captive cetaceans live to 10.7* years old. The information that you don’t get from an average value is that the oldest a captive cetacean has ever lived is 61 years old (and she was born in captivity). This value also doesn’t necessarily give any indication of how long animals in human care today will live. For instance, the average age of living, captive cetaceans is 17.7 years old, a full 7 years older than the average age of animals that have already died.

What do we do next?

There are still over 500 cetaceans living in human care throughout the US. Deciding what to do with them has been a point of contention for animal rights activists and animal care professionals alike. One of the main sticking points for these groups is animal breeding. Should animals be allowed to breed for enrichment, biological imperative, and to maintain the population of captive animals? Or should it be stopped altogether?

We'll start with orcas, specifically, because in March 2016, SeaWorld announced that they were ending the breeding program for their orcas. Whether or not you consider this a victory, one point still stands: there are 24 orcas still living in the US, and these animals live for a long time.

You might be thinking, why not just return these animals to the wild? While this seems both obvious and appealing, people both for and against cetacean captivity tend to agree that most of these animals wouldn’t do well if they were released. There are many factors stacked up against them, like a lack of family group, and not knowing how to hunt or socialize. Assuming that they will need to remain in human care for the rest of their lives, how much longer could we still have captive orcas in the US?

What about sea pens?

One alternative plan that has been discussed a lot recently is to keep animals in “seaside sanctuaries” or “sea pens”. These are essentially nets that enclose a natural lagoon in the ocean. The animals would still need to be fed, trained, and cared for by veterinarians, but they would be in the ocean water.

Those in favor of sea pens claim that experiencing the ocean would act as a sort of “retirement” to animals kept in captivity for long periods of time. Those against sea pens cite that the ocean is home to diseases, pollution, and underwater “noise pollution” that could threaten the captive-born animals. Alternatively, some worry that putting these animals back into the ocean could introduce new problems for wild populations (think antibiotic resistant strains of diseases).

It’s hard to know who is right here and what is best for both the captive and wild animals. But, we do have some data to work with. Some facilities within the US have always housed their animals in sea pens. Are there higher rates of disease-related deaths in sea pen housed animals?
